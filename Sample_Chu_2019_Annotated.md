# Embodied Engagement with Narrative: A Design Framework for Presenting Cultural Heritage Artifacts

**Citation:** Chu, J. H., & Mazalek, A. (2019). Embodied Engagement with Narrative: A Design Framework for Presenting Cultural Heritage Artifacts. *Multimodal Technologies and Interaction*, 3(1), 1.

**Project:** HCI-basic

**Analyzed:** 2026-03-11

# Part 1: Section Summary

## Abstract

The paper proposes the **Tangible and Embodied Narrative Framework (TENF)** for designing interactive museum exhibits that engage visitors with cultural heritage artifacts. The framework consists of three spectra: diegetic vs. non-diegetic, internal vs. external, and ontological vs. exploratory. Findings indicate that embodied engagement through narrative roles helps visitors (1) contextualize artifact meaning and (2) make personalized connections.

## Introduction

The authors address the problem that multi-modal technologies in museums often divert attention away from cultural artifacts. They argue that tangible and embodied interactions, when designed with narrative engagement in mind, can help visitors comprehend cultural context. The paper presents TENF as a design framework and demonstrates its application through two projects: Mapping Place (pilot) and Multi-Sensory Prayer Nuts (case study).

## Literature Review

### Interactive Narrative
- Ryan's framework: internal vs. external (character involvement), ontological vs. exploratory (plot involvement)
- Murray's concept: "diegetic" objects that exist in both physical and virtual worlds
- Diegetic vs. non-diegetic distinction from film studies

### Tangible and Embodied Narrative Systems
- Data-centered view: tangible representation of digital data (Ullmer & Ishii)
- Expressive-movement-centered view: human body as shaping embodied engagement
- Projects: Triangles, Nintendo Wiimote, Tangible Spatial Narratives, NOOT

### Toward a Narrative Framework
Three narrative design elements identified:
1. Physical engagement
2. Narrative role
3. Narrative consequence

## TENF Framework

### Three Spectra:
1. **Diegetic vs. Non-Diegetic** — Interactor's physical position within narrative environment
2. **Internal vs. External** — Narrative perspective (first-person vs. omniscient)
3. **Ontological vs. Exploratory** — How interactor interacts with plot (alter vs. navigate)

### Structure:
- Eight possible combinations of spectra
- Each combination represents different narrative engagement type
- Projects can be mapped onto continuum of each spectrum

## Case Studies

### Mapping Place (2013-2014)
- Pilot project emulating Lukasa board (Luba peoples, Congo)
- Multi-touch tabletop with tangible shells for storytelling
- **Evaluation:** 14 primary school children in groups of 2-4
- **Finding:** Cultural gap hindered visitors' ability to make sense of concepts; interface successful as storytelling app but lacked embodied experience of cultural practice
- **TENF mapping:** Non-diegetic, external, ontological

### Multi-Sensory Prayer Nuts (2014-2016)
- Case study focused on 16th-century Dutch prayer nuts
- Three prototypes: Visual Voyage, Experiencing Spirituality, Scents of Power
- **Evaluation:** 16 participants in lab setting
- **Finding:** Diegetic, internal, exploratory interaction helped participants imagine original context and make personal connections
- **TENF mapping:** Diegetic, internal, exploratory

## Discussion

**Findings:**
1. Embodied engagement through narrative role helps visitors contextualize artifact meaning
2. Visitors make personalized connections to artifacts
3. Different spectrum positions yield different engagement types

**Design Recommendations:**
1. Simulate cultural practices
2. Associate visitors with cultural perspectives
3. Provide simultaneous digital feedback

**Limitations:**
1. Limited to one combination (diegetic, internal, exploratory)
2. Fixed positions on spectra (interactors can move along spectrum during interaction)
3. Designer-centric perspective only
4. Lab setting rather than museum context

## Conclusion

TENF provides overarching conceptual model for designing tangible and embodied narrative experiences. The framework can guide designers in integrating digital media, narrative, and embodiment to engage visitors with cultural heritage content.

## Reader's Critical Notes

### POC (Points of Critique)
- The framework was only tested with one combination of spectra (diegetic, internal, exploratory)—limited generalizability
- Evaluations were conducted in lab settings rather than authentic museum contexts, raising questions about ecological validity
- The framework focuses primarily on designer perspective without incorporating visitor feedback or co-design approaches
- The paper doesn't adequately address how interactors might move along spectra during a single interaction experience

### MOP (Missed Obvious Points)
- No discussion of accessibility considerations for visitors with disabilities
- Limited consideration of cultural sensitivity when designing embodied interactions with artifacts from colonized peoples
- The "cultural gap" identified in Mapping Place evaluation could have been more deeply theorized in relation to decolonial museum studies
- The framework could benefit from connection to existing HCI paradigm literature (e.g., Harrison et al.'s three Paradigms)

### RPP (Relevant Points to Pursue)
- How might TENF apply to intangible cultural heritage (e.g., music, dance, oral traditions)?
- Could the framework be extended to include a fourth spectrum for social/collaborative vs. individual interaction?
- What are the ethical considerations when creating embodied simulations of sacred or culturally sensitive practices?
- How might AI/ML integration enhance or complicate the TENF applications?

### WIL (Logical Extensions)
- Can the TENF framework resolve the tension between authenticity and accessibility in cultural heritage displays?
- How might the framework apply to virtual/mixed reality contexts beyond tangible interfaces?
- Can combining multiple spectrum positions within a single exhibit create more engaging visitor experiences?
